Biological Molecules

Types of Biological Molecules

There are 3 main types of biological molecules in living organisms.

  • Carbohydrates
  • Proteins
  • Lipids

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are made of only 3 obvious elements.

  • Carbon
  • Hydrogen
  • Oxygen

There are different types of carbohydrates that are classified by their complexity.

  • Monosaccharides
    • Glucose
    • Fructose
  • Disaccharides
    • Maltose
    • Sucrose
  • Polysaccharides
    • Starch
    • Glycogen

Monosaccharides are simple sugars made up of a single sugar molecule. All monosaccharides are reducing sugars.

Proteins

Proteins are made up of a sequence of amino acids. A different order of amino acids produces a different protein. Proteins are essential for growth. Proteins comprise of 4 elements.

  • Carbon
  • Hydrogen
  • Oxygen
  • Nitrogen

Proteins can sometimes contain sulphur.

Lipids

Lipids are also called fats. Lipids are made of 2 molecules.

  • Glycerol
  • 3 Fatty Acids

Lipids are also made of the 3 classic elements.

  • Carbon
  • Hydrogen
  • Oxygen

Enzymes

Enzymes are biological catalysts and proteins. Enzymes increase the rate of reaction without being used up. Enzymes have the job of converting substrate molecules into product molecules.

  • Substrate: the reactant molecules that bind to the enzyme
  • Product: the finished product of the reaction

The Active Site

All enzymes have an active site that is specific to a particular substrate.

  • The shape of the active site is complementary to the shape of the substrate
  • A different substrate can't bind to the active site of an enzyme if their shapes do not match
  • Changing the shape of the active site will prevent it from converting substrate into product

Denatured Enzymes

The denaturation of an enzyme is irreversible. It significantly reduces the rate of reaction.

  1. Enzyme begins to denature due to a factor
  2. The shape of the active site changes
  3. The substrate can no longer fit into the active site
  4. The enzyme can no longer catalyse the reaction
  5. This causes the rate of reaction to decrease

Temperature

Increasing temperature typically increases the rate of reaction.

  • An increase in temperature causes an increase in the kinetic energy of substrate molecules and enzymes
  • The substrate and enzymes collide more frequently
  • The enzyme converts the most substrate into product at its optimum temperature

The optimum temperature is where the rate of reaction peaks!

However, high temperatures can cause enzymes to denature.

  1. The enzyme denatures due to high temperature
  2. The shape of the active site changes
  3. The substrate can no longer fit into the active site
  4. The enzyme can no longer catalyse the reaction
  5. The rate of reaction decreases

Alkalinity and Acidity

A solution that is too alkaline or too acidic can denature the enzyme. Unlike temperature, low pH and high pH will denature an enzyme.

Although most enzymes have an optimum pH of 7 (neutral), the optimum pH of the protease enzyme pepsin is very acidic. This means that a neutral pH would likely denature pepsin.
