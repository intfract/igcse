Cells

Organisms

There are 7 characteristics of living organisms. Cells are responsible for carrying out these processes.

  • Movement
  • Respiration
  • Sensitivity
  • Growth
  • Reproduction
  • Excretion
  • Nutrition

Movement

This is an action by an organism or a part of an organism that causes a change in position or place.

Respiration

This is the breakdown of nutrient molecules to release energy for metabolism.

⚠
Respiration is not breathing. Mechanical ventilation refers to the movement of the respiratory system while breathing.

Sensitivity

This is the ability to detect changes or stimuli in the environment and respond appropriately. Neurons are involved in the process.

Growth

This is a permanent increase in size or dry mass by an increase in cell number or size.

Reproduction

This is the process of producing new offspring of the same kind of organism. There are two types.

  • Sexual (Genetically Different Offspring)
  • Asexual (Genetically Identical Offspring)

Excretion

This is the process of removing waste products of metabolism and toxic material.

⚠
Excretion is not egestion. Egestion is the removal of undigested food particles. Excretion includes peeing and sweating.

Nutrition

This is the intake of food for the absorption of nutrients. The digestive system is involved in this process.

Cell Structure

Plant cells and animal cells have distinct features that make them distinguishable.

Plant Cell

Plant cells have 3 organelles that animal cells lack.

  • Cell Wall
  • Chloroplast
  • Vacuole (Large)

The cell wall is fully permeable unlike the cell membrane which is partially permeable.

Animal Cell

Rarely, some animal cells have small vacuoles. However, the main difference is that animal cells have an irregular shape due to the lack of a cell wall.

Cell Processes

Diffusion

Diffusion is the movement of particles from a region of high to low concentration due to Brownian motion or the random movement of particles. Diffusion is a passive process.

Some factors increase the rate of diffusion and some factors decrease the rate of diffusion.

  • Temperature
  • Concentration Gradient
  • Surface Area
  • Diffusion Distance

Osmosis

Osmosis is the movement of water molecules from a region of high to low water potential through a partially permeable membrane. Osmosis is a passive process.

Hypertonic and Hypotonic Solutions

A hypertonic solution has a high concentration and low water potential. A hypotonic solution has a low concentration and high water potential. Concentration is a measure of how much of a substance is dissolved in water in the context of osmosis. The terms hypertonic and hyoptonic are relative to the cell.

Hypertonic Solution

Water leaves the plant or animal cell via osmosis because the water potential inside the cell is greater than the water potential of the solution.

Plasmolysis occurs in plant cells when a large amount of water has left the cytoplasm. This loss of water causes the cytoplasm to shrink and the cell membrane to peel away from the cell wall.

Crenation occurs in animal cells. The loss of water causes the animal cell to shrink and become spiky. This is largely because the surface area of the cell remains constant while the volume decreases.

Hypotonic Solution

Water enters the plant or animal cell via osmosis because the water potentian inside the cell is less than the water potential of the solution.

Turgidity is a state achieved by plant cells that are full with water. Something is turgid when it is stiff. No reference was intended. Plant cells do not burst because they have a strong cellulose cell wall.

Lysis is the process of an animal cell getting nuked due to excessive swelling. The animal cell bursts when the water pressure inside the cell is too high.

Cell Specialisation

Some cells may have a greater or fewer number of cell organelles depending on their role in the organism. For example, not all plant cells will have chloroplasts.

Cells may develop adaptations.

Ciliated Cells

  • Cilia: pushing mucus

Root Hair Cells

  • Large Surface Area: absorption of water and mineral ions
  • Large Vacuole: storage of water

Palisade Mesophyll Cells

  • Many Chloroplasts: photosynthesis

Red Blood Cells

  • Haemoglobin: transport of oxygen

Sperm and Egg Cells

  • Flagellum: swimming (sperm)
  • Jelly Coating: protection (egg)
